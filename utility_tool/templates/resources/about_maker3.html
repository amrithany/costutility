About DecisionMaker

This free, online tool was developed for education decision-makers by the Center for Benefit-Cost Studies of Education at Teachers College, Columbia University (CBCSE) with funding from the William T. Grant Foundation.

The decision-making framework used in DecisionMaker is based on Multiattribute Utility Theory (MAUT) as developed by Edwards (1971), Edwards, Guttentag and Snapper (1975), and Edwards and Newman (1982). The DecisionMaker User Manual includes details on the methods. CBCSE worked with a state education agency, three school districts, three individual schools, and many current and aspiring school leaders in a principal preparation program at Teachers College to adapt the framework for educational contexts.

The purpose of DecisionMaker is to facilitate school, district, or state education agency decisions that require a choice between alternative educational programs, strategies, or tools, for example, when choosing among several math curricula or digital devices, or when considering several scale up/scale down scenarios. Decision-makers are invited to identify factors important in distinguishing between alternative solution options and to gather evidence to determine which solution option best meets stakeholder needs. By juxtaposing the quantified value of each solution option against its costs, DecisionMaker allows you to review the trade-offs between monetary investments and stakeholder values.

You can use DecisionMaker to work through a decision alone or in collaboration with stakeholders, to facilitate multiple ongoing decisions, and to find resources and guidance to help support the decision-making process.

Steps in the decision-making process:
 I Identify the decision to be made

 II Identify stakeholders to participate in the decision-making process

 III Identify Solution Options

 IV Apply screening criteria to the list of Solution Options to create a shortlist of viable options

 V Create a list of evaluation criteria, i.e., factors that will influence the decision

 VI Assign “Importance Scores” to each criterion

 VII Identify measures to assess the extent to which each Solution Option satisfies each criterion

 VIII Collect data to evaluate each Solution Option

 IX Estimate the costs per participant of implementing each Solution Option

 X Use the resulting cost and “utility” values to inform your decision

The Resources & Guidance tab in the black bar at the top of each page includes suggestions and advice about each of these steps.
